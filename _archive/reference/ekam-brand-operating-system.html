Prologue

How to read this manual.

This is v2.0 of the Ekam Brand Operating System. It supersedes the previous Logo & Application Guidelines and consolidates the founder draft (v3.0) with the directional shifts proposed in v3.1. Where v3.0 and v3.1 disagree, v3.0 holds until founder sign-off on the v3.1 shifts.

Eleven volumes. The mark sits inside Volume IV — not at the front. The front is philosophy, audience, and the architecture of the brand itself. The mark serves; it does not lead.

Read slowly. The discipline is the product.

On materials. This document sets the register — local stone, indigenous hardwood, natural mineral wall finish, regional handwoven textile, oxidised iron, unfinished brass, hand-thrown ceramic. The Sustainability Roadmap & Founder Letter sets the source — which quarry, which mill, which forest, which weaver, which chain-of-custody, per site, per season. Where the two touch, the Roadmap governs. The BOS does not promise specific materials; it promises a register the Roadmap is responsible for filling.

Philosophy & Position
The position the brand operates from. The decisions that earn the right to silence everything else.
Chapter 01 — Manifesto

A fixed text.

Ekam is the chance to stop scrolling past beautiful places and finally sit inside one.

Real landscapes. Real light. Real silence. Without the gloss of luxury or the burden of performance.

A stay is not a service to be delivered. It is a setting to be quietly maintained, while the guest does the work of being there.

We are a hospitality brand. We borrow vocabulary from luxury, wellness, adventure — but the centre of gravity is hospitality, in its oldest sense: shelter, warmth, food, a door that closes, a host who knows when to leave.

The job is not to be the most expensive option in the forest. It is to be the most considered one.

No two stays are the same. No two cabins are alike. Because no two pieces of land are alike. We do not standardise the room. We standardise the discipline.

The Singular.
Chapter 02 — The strategic frame

Rich crave nature. Our advantage is the permissions.

India's wealth is climbing faster than its access to wilderness.

Money buys a better hotel. It does not buy a permit to build inside a buffer zone, on a forest department lease, on a tribal grant, on a single-track road that took five signatures and twenty years of relationships to earn.

That is our moat. Ekam exists where most hospitality cannot legally go. We do not need to compete on thread count or chandeliers. We compete on the door — and what the door opens into.

The job, then, is not to be the most expensive option in the forest. It is to be the most considered one — designed, hosted, and run with such care that the privilege of the location feels deserved by the guest, not flexed at them.

The permission moat is the brand. Everything else is craft.
Chapter 03 — The three pillars

Singular. Serene. Wild.

Every design and operational decision should carry at least one of these. If it carries none, cut it.

एकम्
Singular
No two stays are the same. Every cabin, tent, or pod is placed with intent and designed around the land it sits on. Ekam is Sanskrit for the one — we build it into every detail.
शान्ति
Serene
Quiet by design. Guests come for the long exhale, the cup of tea at 6am, the walk that leads nowhere and is the whole point. Our voice mirrors that calm.
वन्य
Wild
Every Ekam is off-grid, off-map, and on its own. We choose the hardest-to-reach places on purpose, because the reward of arrival is the entire experience.
Chapter 04 — Three foundations

Three questions every decision must pass.

Before any decision — architectural, editorial, operational, commercial — three questions are asked. The decision must pass all three.

  1. Is it land-led?

    Does the land decide first, or are we forcing a design onto the site? If the answer requires us to reshape the land, the design is wrong.

  2. Is it operationally honest?

    Are we scrubbing the word luxury from guest-facing surfaces? Are we making staff work invisibly, or asking the guest to perform the work of being in a luxury hotel? Honest service does not announce itself.

  3. Is it silent?

    Are we removing what gets in the way? Every brand decision should subtract. Restraint is the discipline.

Chapter 05 — What we are / What we are not

A short clarification.

What we are

  • A hospitality brand in the oldest sense — shelter, warmth, food, a door that closes.
  • A network of singular cabins on land that other hospitality cannot legally access.
  • A host who knows when to leave.
  • The most considered option in the forest, not the most expensive.
  • A setting to be quietly maintained while the guest does the work of being there.

What we are not

  • A luxury brand. We borrow vocabulary from it; we do not centre on it.
  • A wellness brand. No retreats, no fasts, no transformation packages.
  • An adventure brand. No guided trekking, no off-road experiences.
  • A content brand. We post once a week, maximum.
  • A franchise. Each cabin is the first cabin again.

Chapter 07 — What we will never become

A quarterly check.

Read this list every quarter. If we have started becoming any of these, the brand is in trouble.

  1. A loud-luxury brand whispering expensively at $2000+ a night.
  2. A wellness-content brand selling silent retreats, fasting protocols, or transformation packages.
  3. A franchise. Even one franchise dilutes the model irreversibly.
  4. A brand that posts daily on Instagram. Once a week is the maximum.
  5. A brand that runs influencer campaigns. We do not pay for content.
  6. A brand that lists on Booking-style aggregators with discounted rates.
  7. A brand that offers experiences — guided trekking, cooking classes, photography workshops. The cabin is the experience.
  8. A brand that has a signature scent.
  9. A brand that has a customer-loyalty programme.
  10. A brand whose owner is in the photographs.

Chapter 08 — Who we host

The emotional driver.

Our guests are 22-to-45-year-old Indians and inbound travellers — curious, well-read, quietly exhausted by their city lives. They come from design, technology, media, finance, wellness, and independent practice. They buy less and feel more.

Beneath the demographic spread, our audience shares one psychological position: they want a place that meets them as they are, not as their socials present them. The Ekam stay is a permission slip — to be quiet, to be unobserved, to be a little less interesting in public for forty-eight hours.

The jobs they hire Ekam for.

  1. Disconnect without unplugging the self.
  2. Be in nature without roughing it.
  3. Experience a place beyond the photos they have saved.
  4. Feel transformed by Monday morning — but not in a way that requires telling anyone.

Chapter 10 — What unites them

A short statement.

They buy less. They stay longer. They want their hospitality soft and their adventure honest. They value clarity over cleverness, quiet over spectacle, and a good bed over any amenity on a brochure.

They are not booking a glamping weekend. They are booking a better version of themselves for forty-eight to seventy-two hours.

Chapter 11 — Who we do not speak to

A short list. Strictly enforced.

  1. Bachelor and bachelorette parties. Hen and stag groups.
  2. Wedding-photography parties. Pre-wedding shoots.
  3. Corporate offsites larger than eight people.
  4. Influencer campaigns. Content-creation residencies.
  5. Anyone who asks for a discount in their first email.

This is not snobbery. It is matching. The product is built for solitude, and these audiences disrupt the experience for everyone else.

Chapter 14 — Naming conventions

Always Ekam first.

  1. Three words, separated by the Bindu.

    Always Ekam first, tier second, location third. Examples: Ekam Kutir Chitkul, Ekam Shikhar Kalpa, Ekam Chai Nako.

  2. Spoken aloud as Ekam · Kutir · Chitkul.

    The Bindu is the rhythmic pause.

  3. Never skip a level.

    Even internal ops documents follow the full lockup.

  4. Location names in Inter 500, letter-spaced.

    Never in the serif wordmark face on supplementary copy.

  5. Never substitute a hyphen, slash, or pipe for the Bindu.

    Only the clay dot bridges the words.

Chapter 15 — The wordmark

E · KAM.

Bindu Clay dot between E and K, on cap-midline.

Set in Cormorant Garamond 400, all caps, with a clay-orange Bindu between E and K — vertically aligned with the cap-height midline. The Bindu is not decoration. It is the centre of gravity of the mark.

Five master files: wordmark cream, wordmark reverse, E-monogram, bilingual stack, Bindu solo. Internal teams may not regenerate from system fonts. Production files request from the Brand Hub.

Detailed clearspace, scale, misuse, and the social asset suite live in the companion Logo Specification.

Clearspace
Reserve clearspace equal to the height of the letter E on every side. Nothing enters the moat.
Bindu
Always #B4613A. Diameter 13% of cap-height. Sits between E and K on the cap-midline. Never animates.
Minimum scale
24 px wordmark height on screen. 8 mm in print. Embroidered or engraved: 18 mm minimum.
Letterspacing
8% on screen at hero size. 1.5 mm at 30 mm height in print. Never closer than 4 px.
In running text
Set as Ekam. Capital E, lowercase remainder. The Bindu does not appear in prose.
Chapter 16 — Type system

Four faces.

Display
Cormorant Garamond. Light 300 / Regular 400. Wordmark, headlines, chapter openers, signage. Letter-spacing ≥4 px on headlines, ≥8 px on wordmarks.
Storytelling
Fraunces. Italic default. Storytelling, taglines, pull-quotes, email intros. Upright reserved for longer body. Line-height 1.55.
UI & metadata
Inter. 300, 400, 500. UI, booking flow, way-finding, captions. Letterspacing 2–3 px on eyebrows. 0.5 px on body.
Devanagari
Tiro Devanagari Hindi. Regular and italic. Welcome cards, cabin doors, wax seals, section dividers. Never substitutes for English UI.

Hierarchy.

  1. Hero / cover: Cormorant 78–96 px, 14 px letterspacing.
  2. H2 section opener: Cormorant 38–44 px, 1 px letterspacing.
  3. H3: Cormorant 22 px, 1 px letterspacing.
  4. H4 / eyebrow: Inter 11 px uppercase, 4 px letterspacing, clay colour.
  5. Body: Fraunces or Raleway 15–17 px, line-height 1.55–1.85.
  6. Pull-quote: Fraunces 21 px italic, 1.5 line-height.
  7. Caption / spec: Inter 12–13 px, 1 px letterspacing, moss colour.

Chapter 18i — Misuse

What we never do.

The list below is illustrative, not exhaustive. When in doubt, request approval from the Brand Hub.

  1. Do not float the Bindu above the K.

    The dot sits on the cap-midline, between E and K. Never above any letter.

  2. Do not recolor the Bindu.

    Always #B4613A. Never another colour. Never with opacity.

  3. Do not stretch, condense, or distort.

    Scale by wordmark height — never by container width.

  4. Do not substitute another typeface.

    Cormorant Garamond Regular only. Sans-serif system fonts violate the master.

  5. Do not place on non-canon backgrounds.

    Cream, Sand, Deep Forest, Forest Ink only. Never on Moss, Mist, or photography.

  6. Do not replace the Bindu with a symbol.

    Heart, leaf, flower, emoji, photograph — the dot is fixed in shape.

  7. Do not append marketing taglines under the wordmark.

    The three approved taglines are the only verbal companions to the wordmark, and they appear in specified contexts only.

  8. Do not surface operational tier names in guest-facing lockups.

    Standard / Premium / Luxury are backend only. Never in lockups, signage, or marketing copy.

  9. Do not append descriptors below the wordmark.

    "living", "retreats", "cabins" — pre-v3 forms are deprecated. The wordmark is complete.

Chapter 19 — Land doctrine

Site selection.

The land is not a backdrop. It is the project. We choose the site first; we design the cabin second.

  1. Built area never exceeds 8% of acquired land.

    For a 5-acre parcel, no more than 0.4 acres are built. The remaining 92% is left, restored, or actively rewilded. Non-negotiable.

  2. The cabin disappears at 50 metres.

    From any approach path at 50 m distance, a single cabin should not visibly read against the landscape. If it reads, the cladding, roof line, or siting is wrong.

  3. One tree felled requires three planted.

    Native species only. Planting within the same monsoon window as felling. Per-tree accounting, not per-canopy. Project lead signs the register before construction.

  4. No site requires removal of a perennial water feature.

    Streams, springs, ponds, seasonal water-paths are not redirected, capped, or built over. The cabin moves. The water does not.

  5. The approach is on foot.

    Vehicular access ends at the perimeter. The final approach is on foot — never a paved road to the door.

  6. Sites we do not build on.

    Floodplains. Migration corridors verified by a local biologist. Land within 200 m of a working village without the village's invitation. Sites whose principal view is another building. Sites whose principal sound is a road.

Chapter 20 — Architectural language

Single-room, single-window, single-view.

The architecture is foreground-first: a vessel built to direct attention to one window, one view, one place.

Footprint
One- or two-room cabins. Never more. A second room only if a partitioned sleeping space is required.
Window
One large window per cabin, oriented to the primary view. The rest of the cabin is a vessel for that window.
Roof
Pitch follows local vernacular — the regional building tradition for the elevation, climate, and material register of the site. Per the Sustainability Roadmap's site-specific spec. Never "modern cabin chic."
Foundation
Foundation and stilts are visible. We do not hide structure behind cladding.
Ceiling height
3.5–4.5 m. Never lofted, never double-height. The cabin should feel held.
First sight on entry
The first thing the guest sees on entering is the window — never the bed, never the kitchen.
Disappearance test
Before sign-off, the head architect walks 50 m from the cabin in three directions. Visible / invisible. Visible fails.

Chapter 22 — Lighting

Day, Evening, Night, Off.

Built around four moods and a single capacitive panel per cabin.

Day
No electric lighting on. The window does the work. Unbleached cotton curtains, full-pull only.
Evening
2400–2700K. Three sources only: pendant over the eating surface, wall sconce by the bed, lantern on the porch.
Night
A single 1800K bedside source. No overheads after sunset by default. The wood stove is part of the lighting plan.
Off
Everything off. The window does dawn.
CRI
CRI 95 minimum. We buy on quality of light, not lumens.
Smart panel
One capacitive smart panel per cabin: matte oxidised brass face, four etched zones (Day · Evening · Night · Off). Bulbs locked to 1800–2700K. RGB modes disabled at the controller.

Chapter 23 — Landscape integration

The garden is the forest.

Eleven rules. Each earns its place.

Constraints, not refusals. Each one earns its place because it makes the experience better.

  1. Wi-Fi at the Chai pavilion only.

    One reliable network at the property's Ekam Chai stop. Not in the cabin. Bandwidth is a privilege our locations earn through difficulty.

  2. No television, projector, or smart screen.

    Including in the bathroom mirror.

  3. No room service. No mini-bar. No in-cabin F&B programme.

    The kitchen (where present) is yours, not ours.

  4. No staff inside the cabin once you have checked in.

    The door closes. Housekeeping happens only on departure or by explicit request.

  5. No printed marketing.

    No flyer, no rate card, no laminated breakfast menu.

  6. No QR codes for marketing.

    Anything worth knowing is told in person. QRs only ever for utility (Wi-Fi credentials, lock pairing).

  7. No upsells. No experiences menu.

    No spa, no wellness package, no add-ons. The cabin is the experience.

  8. No music piped through speakers.

    Silence is the soundtrack.

  9. No artificial scent.

    No diffusers, no signature fragrance, no incense by default.

  10. No tourist info packets.

    No "things to do nearby". The place is the thing.

  11. Post-stay: one checkout note and one survey.

    Written like a letter. No re-marketing, no rate-card pushes.

Chapter 52 — Sustainability

Operational, not promotional.

We do not market sustainability. We measure it, and we improve it. The day we run a sustainability campaign, we have stopped doing the work.

Carbon.

  1. Solar primary across all properties — minimum 80% of electricity load by year three of operation.
  2. Wood stoves use locally sourced fallen wood only. Never live-felled timber. A documented chain-of-custody.
  3. Cabin construction uses ≥70% local materials by volume — local stone, indigenous hardwood, natural mineral wall finish, hand-thrown ceramic. Species and source governed by the Sustainability Roadmap site spec.
  4. Carbon accounting per stay — published annually, never on the homepage.

Water.

  1. Rainwater harvesting on every roof. Greywater recycled to garden / forest re-charge wherever soil allows.
  2. Low-flow taps and showers as default. Pressure designed for stillness, not power-shower bombast.
  3. Drinking water from local source, filtered on-site. Never bottled. Glass carafe with a small clay disc on the lid.

Waste.

  1. Zero single-use plastic anywhere on property — including back-of-house, including kitchen storage.
  2. Composting is the default for organic waste, fed back to the property's garden.
  3. Linens and textiles sent to local laundry partners — never industrial detergent, only soap-nut and cold wash.

Community & land.

  1. Every property has a community fund — a fixed percentage of revenue (3% target, 2% floor) returned to the village or panchayat we operate within. Use is determined by the local council, not by us.
  2. Hosts and operations staff hired from within 20 km of the property where possible. Paid above the regional median by a documented margin.
  3. Local artisans (weavers, soap-makers, potters, masons) are named on the in-cabin store hangtags and in the journal. Never anonymised. Never undercut.
  4. Land-use is regenerative or neutral. We do not clear forest. We restore. Every property publishes a habitat report annually.
  5. We do not host activities that disturb wildlife or local rhythms — no off-roading, no "adventure sports" packages.
Chapter 52a — The Sustainability Roadmap handoff

BOS sets the register. Roadmap governs the source.

This is the most important separation in the document. The BOS does not promise specific materials. The Sustainability Roadmap & Founder Letter is the canonical document on every material sourcing, carbon, water, waste, community, and chain-of-custody decision.

The BOS commits to a sensory and material register — what a guest sees, feels, smells, hears, and touches. Local stone underfoot. Indigenous hardwood overhead. Natural mineral on the walls. Hand-thrown ceramic at the table. Region-appropriate handwoven textile in the linens. Oxidised iron at the hand. Unfinished brass at the fire.

The Sustainability Roadmap commits to source — which species of hardwood, which quarry of stone, which artisan kiln, which mill, which weaver's village, which chain-of-custody, which carbon accounting per kg of material, which water budget per fixture, which composting plan per kitchen. The Roadmap is updated annually and per site.

Where the two documents touch, the Roadmap governs the specific. The BOS governs the experience.

Material decisions
All specific material specifications — species, supplier, source radius, chain-of-custody — live in the Sustainability Roadmap. The BOS never names a specific timber, stone, or weave.
Carbon, water, waste
All operational targets, accounting methods, and reporting cadences live in the Roadmap. The BOS commits to the principle (60% solar by year three, rainwater harvesting on every roof, zero single-use plastic); the Roadmap commits to the figures and the timelines per property.
Community fund
The BOS commits to the principle (revenue percentage returned to local council). The Roadmap commits to the percentage by site, the council relationship, the use audit cadence.
Artisan register
Named in the Roadmap. Updated annually. Hangtags, journal credits, and in-cabin store labels draw from the Roadmap register — not from a BOS-side list.
Conflict resolution
If the BOS implies a material the Roadmap cannot source sustainably for a given site, the Roadmap holds. The BOS register adapts. We do not break the Roadmap to fulfil a BOS description.
Sign-off chain
Material register changes in the BOS require Tier 2 (Brand Director) approval. Source changes in the Roadmap require Tier 3 (Founder + Director) approval. The Roadmap sits operationally one layer down but is owned at the highest tier of governance because it is where the brand's hardest commitments are kept.
The brand is a contract with materials. The Roadmap is where that contract is signed.

Three tiers of approval.

Brand integrity is maintained by a clear chain of approval. Anyone may propose; approval lives at three layers.

Tier 01
Host
Day-to-day operations within the cabin and on the property. Welcome cards, menu rotations within the seasonal frame, minor maintenance.
Tier 02
Brand Director
All copy. All campaigns. All partner agreements. Pricing changes within bands. Identity and visual application questions. Vendor selection.
Tier 03
Founder + Director
New properties. New sub-brands or tiers. Removal of any constraint in Chapter 51. Hosted partnership tier. Any change to the wordmark, the Bindu, or this document.

Standing rules.

  1. No constraint in this document may be removed without Tier 3 approval and a written rationale added to the revision log.
  2. No new sub-brand, tier, or property launches without Tier 3 sign-off, including written preconditions and a documented audit against this entire document.
  3. Every property opens with a Section-by-Section audit. Any deviation is documented and approved before launch.

Chapter 56 — Vendor passport

The materials register.

Every supplier signs the same passport before any production order ships.

  1. Local sourcing radius.

    Stone, wood, and primary cladding materials sourced within 50 km of the destination site. Documented at the quarry / mill / forest. Site lead countersigns the source.

  2. No-plastic clause.

    Supplier commits in writing that no plastic — visible, hidden, or transitional — is used in delivered product. Plastic packaging from upstream supply must be removed before delivery.

  3. Hand-finish tolerance.

    Where a material is hand-finished, production tolerances follow craft, not industrial standards. Variance welcomed; uniformity is suspicious. We do not return work for being imperfect.

  4. Labour conditions.

    Every supplier provides a labour audit on annual cadence — fair wage, no child labour, no bonded labour, working conditions documented.

  5. The materials register.

    Every approved supplier sits in the Brand Hub's materials register. Reviewed quarterly. Suppliers who shift practices below standard are removed within one quarter.

  6. Right of refusal.

    Ekam reserves the right to refuse a shipment that does not meet specification. The supplier replaces, at the supplier's cost. Built into every PO.

The brand is a contract with materials, not a contract with marketing.
