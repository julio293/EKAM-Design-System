— 02 · Spatial type अक्षर · the letter at scale

Typography sized for buildings.

The same five typefaces, applied across six physical substrates. Stone, brass, painted plate, paper plaque, vinyl on cream, timber — each with its own legibility minimum, viewing distance, and bilingual stacking rule. Type that survives weather and reads at 15 metres.

Aक्रम · the ramp

Six spatial sizes. Read by distance, not by pixels.

The digital ramp measures in px; the spatial ramp measures in millimetres. Each row is calibrated to a viewing distance — a 12 mm cap-height reads from 1.5 m, an 80 mm cap-height reads from 15 m. Below the minimum, the letter falls apart.

— The 100-rule  A useful approximation: cap height in mm × 100 = max comfortable reading distance in mm. A 30 mm cap reads cleanly at ~3 m; a 100 mm cap reads at ~10 m. Below this the letter blurs; the form survives but the word disappears.

Bद्रव्य · the substrate

Six substrates. Type behaves differently on each.

The same Cormorant Garamond E·KAM, set six ways. Carving subtracts; etching cuts in; painting sits on top; vinyl is a layer; ceramic is fired in. Each substrate has its own minimum size and its own legibility behaviour.

Cद्विभाषा · two languages

Devanagari above, English below.

Where bilingual is load-bearing — cabin doors, the wax seal, the welcome plate — Devanagari sits on top, English below. Optical sizing is matched so both languages read at the same weight. The bindu separator only appears in the English line.

Stacking rules

Devanagari on top. The reading direction in stacked bilingual lockups is top-to-bottom: Devanagari first, English second. This is the opposite of most Indian institutional signage (which puts English on top) and is a small but deliberate stance.

Devanagari is always 1.4× the English cap-height. Optical sizing — Devanagari letters appear visually smaller than Latin letters at the same point size because of the script's vertical compression. The 1.4× multiplier corrects this. Below this ratio, the script looks subordinate.

No bindu in the Devanagari line. The bindu is part of the English wordmark — the separator between E and KAM. The Devanagari एकम् already carries the script's own diacritic (the बिन्दु on the final letter), so adding our brand bindu would be tautological.

Centre-aligned, never left-aligned. The bilingual stack is always centred. Left-aligning Devanagari above left-aligned English creates an optical jaggedness that left-aligned Latin alone does not have.

Eनियम · the rules

Five rules. Apply before fabrication.

— Rule 01

Always Cormorant. Even on stone.

No display-substitute fonts (Trajan, Optima) on stone. The carve is harder; the brand survives the carve. If the carver objects, the carver is wrong.

— Rule 02

No drop shadows. The substrate makes the shadow.

Brass casts a shadow because it's etched in. Stone casts a shadow because it's carved out. Painted plates are flat — and that is correct. Faux depth is not added.

— Rule 03

Tracking +0.08 to +0.12. Always.

Default Cormorant tracking is too tight for stone, brass, and signage. The 0.08–0.12 letter-spacing range is the rule — the brand's quiet authority comes from the air between letters.

— Rule 04

Below 8 mm, switch to Inter.

Cormorant 400 disintegrates below 8 mm cap-height in physical print. The eyebrow tier (spatial/sm and below) is set in Inter Medium — the system's UI face — for legibility, not for branding.

— Rule 05

The bindu scales differently.

In the digital wordmark the bindu is 16% of cap-height. In physical signage above 30 mm cap, the bindu reduces to 10% of cap-height — otherwise it dominates. Never below 6 mm absolute diameter, ever.

— Anti-rule

No engraved fill colour.

Stone carving and brass etching are never paint-filled. The depth carries the legibility. Filling the carve with white paint reads as municipal signage and breaks the register.
